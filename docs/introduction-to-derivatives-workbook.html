9.4 What Is A Derivative?

You‚Äôve probably seen derivatives before‚Äîeven if you didn‚Äôt call them that. If you‚Äôve ever looked at how fast something was growing, declining, or shifting in space or time, you‚Äôve already been thinking about derivatives.

Mathematically, the derivative describes the instantaneous rate of change‚Äîthe slope of the tangent line to a curve at a given point.

9.4.1 Derivative Notation: Speaking the Language of Change

In calculus, there are several common notations used to represent the derivative‚Äîthe instantaneous rate of change of a function. Understanding these notations is key to reading and writing mathematics fluently, especially when interpreting real-world problems in environmental science.

9.4.1.1 Common Derivative Notations

Let‚Äôs say we have a function, we could right this as:

\[ f(x) = something\ with\ x's \ in\ it \\ y= something\ with\ x's \ in\ it \]

We can then write the notation of the derivative with respect to \(x\) in several ways, all of which mean exactly the same thing:

Notation Spoken As Description
\(f'(x)\) ‚Äúf prime of x‚Äù Most compact form; often used in textbooks
\(\frac{dy}{dx}\) ‚Äúdee y over dee x‚Äù Emphasizes that \(y\) is a function of \(x\); Leibniz notation
\(\frac{d}{dx} f(x)\) ‚Äúdee by dee x of f of x‚Äù Shows that differentiation is an operation
\(D_x f(x)\) ‚ÄúD sub x of f of x‚Äù Operator form used in some texts
\(y'\) ‚Äúy prime‚Äù Used when \(y = f(x)\), common in applied settings
  • Prime notation: e.g., \(f'(x)\), \(g'(t)\)
  • Leibniz notation: e.g., \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), \(\frac{d}{dt}\)
  • Operator notation: e.g., \(\frac{d}{dx}(f(x))\), applying \(\frac{d}{dt}\) to an expression

9.4.1.2 The Derivative as an Operator

The notation \(\frac{d}{dx}\) is not just a symbol‚Äîit‚Äôs an operator. That means it acts on a function to produce another function: the derivative.

Think of it this way:

The symbol \(\frac{d}{dx}\) is a command: ‚ÄúTake the derivative of whatever comes after this with respect to \(x\).‚Äù

So if you write:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left(3x^2 + 2x\right) \]

You are instructing the reader (or yourself!) to differentiate the expression \(3x^2 + 2x\) with respect to \(x\). The result:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left(3x^2 + 2x\right) = 6x + 2 \]

9.4.2 Snapshots vs speed of change

We‚Äôve seen examples where snapshots (a single measurement) and speed (how fast something changes) both matter. The derivative is the mathematical tool that lets us capture that ‚Äúspeed of change‚Äù precisely.

9.9 From Average to Instantaneous: The Role of Limits

We want to connect the average rate of change to the instantaneous rate of change.
Start with the average rate formula:

\[ \text{Average Rate on } [a,b] = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a} \]

9.9.1 Scaffold the Derivation

Practice (Step by Step):

  1. Start with the formula for the average rate of change between two points \(a\) and \(b\):
    \[ \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a} \]
    • What does the numerator mean?
    • What does the denominator mean?






  2. Introduce a new variable \(h = b-a\).
    • Rewrite the denominator in terms of \(h\).
    • Replace \(b\) with \(a+h\).






  3. Show that the formula becomes:
    \[ \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h} \]
    • Why is this still the average rate of change, just written differently? Try to explain this with a sketch.







  1. Now imagine shrinking the interval smaller and smaller: let \(h \to 0\). This makes the slope of secant line approach the slope of the tangent line.
    • What problem happens if you just set \(h=0\)?
    • How does the limit fix this?
    • Add in the limit notation





  2. Arrive at the definition of the derivative:
    \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h} \]

9.10 Finding the Derivative Using the Limit Definition of a Derivative

We‚Äôll now practice moving from the definition of the derivative to actually computing one.
Remember, the derivative at a point \(a\) is defined as:

\[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h} \]

We are going to take this one step at a time:

  • We‚Äôll deal with the numerator
  • We‚Äôll add in the fraction term

  • We‚Äôll add the limit

9.10.1 Step 1: Setup the equations

Activity:
Suppose we have the function:

\[ f(x) = x^2 \]

We want to find the derivative at \(x = 2\).

  1. Write down the limit definition of the derivative at \(a = 2\).
  2. Substitute \(a = 2\) into the formula (but don‚Äôt simplify yet).
  3. What expression do you now have?

Notes:






9.10.2 Step 2: Expand the Numerator

Activity:
The numerator is:

\[ f(2+h) - f(2) \]

  1. Compute \(f(2+h)\).
  2. Compute \(f(2)\).
  3. Subtract them to simplify the numerator.
  4. Write your result in expanded form.

Notes:






9.10.3 Step 3: Simplify the Difference Quotient

Activity:
Now substitute your numerator back into:

\[ \frac{f(2+h) - f(2)}{h} \]

  1. Factor/simplify if possible.
  2. What cancels out?
  3. Why is this helpful?

Notes:












9.10.4 Step 4: Take the Limit

Activity:
Finally, take the limit as \(h \to 0\).

  1. Substitute \(h = 0\) after simplification.
  2. What value do you get for \(f'(2)\)?
  3. Interpret: What does this mean in terms of the slope of the tangent line?

Notes:






You‚Äôve found the derivative at a point \(a=2\)

9.10.5 Step 5: Generalize

Activity:
Repeat the same process, but this time use \(x\) instead of a fixed number like 2.

  1. Start with:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

  1. Expand \(f(x+h)\) and subtract \(f(x)\).
  2. Simplify and take the limit.
  3. What is the general derivative function of \(f(x) = x^2\)?

Notes:












You‚Äôve now found the derivative function
